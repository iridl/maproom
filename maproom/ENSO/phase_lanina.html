La Niña

During La Nina conditions, the westward flow present during neutral conditions is intensified.

This condition is responsible for anomalies in sea surface temperature; above average in the western Pacific and below average in the eastern Pacific. In accordance with the shifts in sea surface temperature, rainfall will be relatively higher in the western Pacific and relatively lower in the eastern Pacific.

During La Nina, the deviations of sea surface temperature and rainfall from normal are generally less dramatic than the deviations that occur during El Nino.

During La Nina, the following conditions are likely

  • Surface Water Circulation Enhanced Westward flow- The sea surface waters are anomalously warmer in the western Pacific than in the eastern Pacific. This creates an enhanced thermal current near the surface (dense (cold) trying to go to less dense (warm)).

  • Lower Atmospheric Circulation Enhanced Westward flow- The current of water that is near the sea surface creates a direct thermal circulation in the lower atmosphere towards the west. Essentially, the waters just below and at the surface are moving west faster than average and the winds right above the surface are enhanced, moving west at a greater magnitude.

  • Upper Atmospheric Circulation Enhanced Eastward flow- The lower level circulation towards the west creates a situation where there is colder water and air being brought into a region of warmer water and air. When the cold air and water hits the warmer air and water it evaporates and forms into clouds and eventually precipitation. Once the clouds form, the air parcel rises and leads to a reverse circulation in the upper atmosphere towards the east. During La Nina, this cycle is enhanced.

  • Thermocline Slanted down from the eastern Pacific to the western Pacific with a slope steeper than average. The thermocline marks the furthest extent of upward movement of deep, cold water under the ocean. Even though the thermocline doesn't reach the surface, it comes closer in some areas (like the eastern Pacific) than in others (western Pacific), leading to the eastern Pacific usually experiencing colder water temperatures near the surface than the western Pacific

The La Nina of 1998

In January 1998, we were at the peak of an El Niño event. In May, the eastern Pacific temperature anomaly (SSTA) dropped rapidly, in part because the actual sea surface temperature (SST) decreased, and in part because the climatological sea surface temperature (which is removed from the SST to form the anomaly) increases in May. By June, the SSTA shows a good-sized cold spot, though the water to the east is still warm and thus the NINO3 index (150W-90W 5S-5N average SSTA) is barely negative.
