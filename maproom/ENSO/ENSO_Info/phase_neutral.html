Neutral

During ENSO neutral conditions trade winds blow westward across the equatorial Pacific Ocean. Blowing against the oceanâ€™s surface, these winds result in a westward current.

These persistent winds and currents are responsible for sea levels in the western Pacific, for example, in Indonesia, to be up to 50 cm higher than those in the eastern Pacific, for example, in Peru.

In addition, neutral conditions also foster upwelling of cool, nutrient-rich sea water on the northern Pacific coast of South America. This process supports healthy marine ecosystem and promotes robust aquaculture activities. Compared to other equatorial Pacific regions, relatively cool sea temperatures will extend along the equatorial Pacific from South America to the central Pacific contributing to relatively dry conditions. Areas in the western Pacific ocean regions will experience higher than average rainfall as the increase in warm surface water leads to convection and relatively abundant rainfall.

During ENSO Neutral, the following conditions are likely

  • Vulnerability: Socioeconomic indicators are monitored continuously and determine the vulnerability of a specific region. These indicators are especially useful in predicting the severity of potential outbreaks. Examples of such indicators may be low immunity, malnutrition, HIV, population movement and drug resistance.

  • Surface Water Circulation Towards the West- Under neutral conditions, the sea surface waters are warmer in the western Pacific than in the eastern Pacific. This creates a thermal current near the surface (dense (cold) trying to go to less dense (warm)).

  • Lower Atmospheric Circulation Towards the West- The current of water that is near the sea surface creates a direct thermal circulation in the lower atmosphere towards the west. Essentially, the waters just below and at the surface are moving west and the winds right above the surface are moving west in normal conditions.

  • Upper Atmospheric Circulation Towards the East- The lower level circulation towards the west creates a situation where there is colder water and air being brought into a region of warmer water and air. When the cold air and water hits the warmer air and water it evaporates and forms into clouds and eventually precipitation. Once the clouds form, the air parcel rises and leads to a reverse circulation in the upper atmosphere towards the east.

  • Thermocline Slanted down from the eastern Pacific to the western Pacific. The thermocline marks the furthest extent of upward movement of deep, cold water under the ocean. Even though the thermocline doesn't reach the surface, it comes closer in some areas (like the eastern Pacific) than in others (western Pacific), leading to the eastern Pacific usually experiencing colder water temperatures near the surface than the western Pacific
