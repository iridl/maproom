La Niña

Much like El Niño, La Niña is another extreme phase of the climate oscillation known as ENSO (short for El Niño Southern Oscillation). In this phase, the central equatorial Pacific becomes much colder than normal, signified by the deeper blue in the animation.

These winds move water westward, and consequently sea level is higher than normal in the west and lower in the east. Equatorial Sea Surface Temperature (SST) is slightly warm in the west and somewhat cold in the east.

The La Nina of 1998

In January 1998, we were at the peak of an El Niño event. In May, the eastern Pacific temperature anomaly (SSTA) dropped rapidly, in part because the actual sea surface temperature (SST) decreased, and in part because the climatological sea surface temperature (which is removed from the SST to form the anomaly) increases in May. By June, the SSTA shows a good-sized cold spot, though the water to the east is still warm and thus the NINO3 index (150W-90W 5S-5N average SSTA) is barely negative.
